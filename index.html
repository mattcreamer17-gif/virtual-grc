01

The opening

You built this on 20+ years of GRC work, then packaged it as vGRCNOW.

The platform now covers eight frameworks including ISO 42001. The 2025 eMerge booth led on CMMC preparedness for the defense industrial base. The marketing calls the product AI-driven. A procurement team at a DoD prime will ask what that actually means, and the answer has to hold up in writing.

Most GRC products that lead on AI are solving three different problems at the same time, and treating them as one feature.

Problem 1

Policy drafting

This is a generative AI problem. A model writes text against a control. Humans edit. The loop is familiar and well-understood.

Problem 2

Evidence matching

This is a rules-and-retrieval problem. The control exists. The evidence exists. The work is tying them together with provenance.

Problem 3

Gap analysis

This is a structured-scoring problem. Controls have criteria. Criteria have weights. A model is not the right tool for half the judgments.

02

The 60 / 30 / 10 view

Most of what lives under an AI-driven compliance product is not AI.

60% Traditional code and database queries

Policy storage, user roles, audit logs, evidence versioning, access reviews. Postgres does this faster, cheaper, and with better guarantees than any LLM.

30% Rule-based logic against framework controls

CMMC 2.0 has 110 controls. NIST 800-171 has 110. The mapping between them is deterministic. A rules engine makes this legible to an auditor. A model muddies it.

10% Genuine AI where model judgment adds value

Novel policy language. Control-to-evidence semantic matching across ambiguous documents. ISO 42001 narrative reviews. This is the real AI surface.

A clean orchestration layer makes each layer legible.

Legible to your team. Legible to your customer's auditor. Legible to the ISO 42001 reviewer who will eventually show up. This is the consulting we do. A one-week audit of the orchestration layer, a map of which features belong on which layer, and a short list of what to rebuild and what to leave alone.
